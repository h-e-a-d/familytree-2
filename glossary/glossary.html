Genealogy Glossary

Master the language of family history research with our comprehensive collection of genealogical terms and definitions.

A

Abstract
An abbreviated transcription of a document that includes the date of the record and every name it contains. May also provide relationships or descriptions of the people mentioned.
Affinity
A relationship created by marriage. It refers to the connection between a person and their spouse's blood relatives, or between a person and their relative's spouse.
Ahnentafel
A German word meaning "ancestor table." This numbering system assigns each ancestor a number, making organization easier. The subject is #1, father #2, mother #3, etc.
Ancestor
A person from whom one is descended, typically more distant than grandparents. Includes parents, grandparents, great-grandparents, and earlier generations.
Ancestry
One's family or ethnic descent; the line of people from whom a person is descended. Also refers to the collective ancestors of a family.
Autosomal DNA
DNA inherited from both parents that can help identify relatives within about 5-7 generations. Used in modern DNA testing for genealogy.

B

Banns
Public announcements of an intended marriage, traditionally made in church on three successive Sundays before the wedding ceremony.
Birth Record
An official document recording the birth of a person, typically including name, date, place of birth, and parents' names.
Brick Wall
A genealogical term for a research obstacle where no additional information can be found about an ancestor, creating a dead end in research.

C

Census
An official count and survey of a population, typically including names, ages, occupations, and family relationships. Conducted periodically by governments.
Centimorgan (cM)
A unit of measurement for genetic distance and DNA matching. Used to determine how closely related two people are based on shared DNA.
Circa (c.)
Latin term meaning "about" or "approximately," used to indicate an estimated date when the exact date is unknown.
Collateral Line
Family members who are not direct ancestors or descendants, such as siblings, aunts, uncles, and cousins.
Consanguinity
Relationship by blood; kinship through descent from a common ancestor. The degree of blood relationship between two people.

D

Death Record
Official documentation of a person's death, including date, place, cause of death, and often family information.
Descendant
A person's offspring in any degree; children, grandchildren, great-grandchildren, and subsequent generations.
Direct Line
The ancestral line that follows a direct path from parent to child, excluding siblings and their descendants.
DNA
Deoxyribonucleic acid; the genetic material inherited from parents that can be used to determine family relationships and ethnic origins.
Dower
The legal right of a widow to a portion of her deceased husband's estate, typically one-third of his real property.

E

Emigrant
A person who leaves their country or region to settle permanently in another place.
Estate
The total property, real and personal, owned by an individual, especially as left after death.
Extraction
The process of copying genealogical information from original documents or records for research purposes.

F

Family Group Sheet
A form used to record information about a family unit, including parents, their marriage details, and all children with their vital statistics.
Family Tree
A chart showing family relationships in a tree structure, displaying ancestors, descendants, and collateral relatives.
First Cousin
The child of one's aunt or uncle; someone who shares grandparents but not parents with you.

G

Gazetteer
A geographical dictionary providing information about places, including historical names, locations, and administrative changes.
GEDCOM
GEnealogy Data COMmunications; a standardized file format for exchanging genealogical data between different software programs.
Genealogy
The study and tracing of family lineages and histories; the practice of researching and documenting family relationships.
Generation
All people born and living at approximately the same time; a single step in the line of descent from an ancestor.

H

Heritage
Something inherited from predecessors; cultural traditions, customs, and genetic traits passed down through generations.
Homestead
A home and surrounding land owned by a family, especially land acquired under the Homestead Act in the United States.

I

Immigrant
A person who comes to live permanently in a foreign country from their country of origin.
Index
An alphabetical list of names or subjects with references to where they can be found in documents or records.
Intestate
The condition of dying without leaving a valid will; property is distributed according to law rather than the deceased's wishes.
Issue
Offspring; children, grandchildren, and all direct descendants of a person.

J

Julian Calendar
The calendar system used from 46 BC to about 1582, named after Julius Caesar. Often called "Old Style" dating in genealogical records.

K

Kinship
Family relationship; connection by blood, marriage, or adoption between individuals.

L

Lineage
Direct descent from an ancestor; ancestry or pedigree, especially when traced through a particular line.
Locality
A specific geographic area or place, particularly important in genealogy for understanding where ancestors lived.

M

Maiden Name
A woman's family name before marriage, in cultures where women traditionally take their husband's surname upon marriage.
Marriage Record
Official documentation of a marriage, including names of bride and groom, date, place, and often parents' names and witnesses.
Maternal Line
The ancestral line traced through the mother's side of the family, following female ancestors.
Microfilm
A method of preserving documents by photographing them onto film at reduced size, commonly used for historical records.
Migration
The movement of people from one place to another, either within a country or internationally, often tracked in genealogical research.
Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA)
DNA inherited only from the mother, useful for tracing maternal lineages over many generations.

N

Naturalization
The legal process by which a foreign citizen becomes a citizen of a new country, often documented in genealogical records.
Née
French term meaning "born," used to indicate a woman's maiden name (the name she was born with).
Next of Kin
The closest living blood relative of a person, typically used in legal contexts for inheritance or decision-making purposes.

O

Obituary
A published notice of someone's death, often including biographical information and surviving family members.
Oral History
Historical information transmitted by word of mouth, including family stories, traditions, and memories passed down through generations.

P

Passenger List
A record of passengers on a ship, train, or other transport, valuable for tracking immigration and migration patterns.
Paternal Line
The ancestral line traced through the father's side of the family, following male ancestors.
Pedigree
A record of ancestry showing direct line of descent; a family tree focusing on direct ancestors only.
Primary Source
Original documents or first-hand records created at the time of an event, considered the most reliable genealogical evidence.
Probate
The legal process of validating a will and distributing a deceased person's estate, often providing valuable genealogical information.
Progenitor
The earliest known ancestor in a family line; the founding ancestor of a lineage or family.

Q

Quaker Records
Religious records kept by the Society of Friends (Quakers), known for their detailed documentation of births, deaths, and marriages.

R

Relationship
The connection between family members, whether by blood, marriage, or adoption.
Repository
A place where documents, records, or other materials are stored, such as archives, libraries, or museums.
Research Log
A systematic record of genealogical research activities, including sources searched, findings, and areas to investigate further.

S

Secondary Source
Information derived from primary sources, such as compiled genealogies, published family histories, or biographical sketches.
Siblings
Brothers and sisters; children who share the same parents or at least one parent in common.
Social Security Death Index (SSDI)
A database of death records for people who had Social Security numbers and whose deaths were reported to the Social Security Administration.
Source Citation
A reference that identifies where specific genealogical information was found, essential for verifying and sharing research.
Surname
A family name; the name shared by family members, typically passed down from father to children.

T

Tombstone
A grave marker or headstone, often containing valuable genealogical information such as birth and death dates.
Transcription
The process of copying text exactly as it appears in an original document, preserving spelling and punctuation.

U

Unmarried
Not married; single status, often abbreviated as "unm." in historical records.

V

Vital Records
Official records of life events including birth certificates, marriage licenses, divorce decrees, and death certificates.

W

Will
A legal document expressing a person's wishes for the distribution of their property after death, often containing family information.
Widow
A woman whose husband has died and who has not remarried.
Widower
A man whose wife has died and who has not remarried.

Y

Y-DNA
DNA from the Y chromosome, passed from father to son, useful for tracing paternal lineages.
